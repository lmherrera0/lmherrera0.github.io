AI sometimes makes things up. It sounds confident, uses proper grammar, and presents “facts” that turn out to be completely fabricated. In professional contexts — client communications, executive reports, regulatory submissions — this creates real risk.

“Nothing passes unverified.”

GGP is a set of instructions you give to any AI that forces it to be honest about what it actually knows versus what it is guessing. Instead of getting polished content that might contain hidden nonsense, you get a draft where every uncertainty is visible. You fill the gaps, accept or reject the assumptions, then the AI produces the final clean version.

◆ How It Works

Every statement the AI makes gets a visual marker — a transparent label showing its verification status.

CONFIRMED “I checked this — here is the source” GAP “I do not have this information” INFERENCE “I am assuming this — you should verify” UNVERIFIED “I tried but could not confirm” RISK “This could get you in trouble”

Not All Sources Are Equal

Just because something is “cited” does not mean it is true. A random blog is very different from an SEC filing. GGP uses a four-tier system so you always know source quality.

You Decide the Depth

After each question round, choose: answer and continue, produce with what the AI has, or go deeper. The framework adapts to your needs, not the other way around.

◆ GGP Visual Markers

Tag every claim in your content. The citation format below must accompany every CONFIRMED marker.

CONFIRMED

Verified with Tier 1–3 source. Full citation required.

[CONFIRMED: Source | Tier X | Location | Date | URL]
  • Specific claim appears in the source
  • Data current (within 1–2 years)
  • Tier 4 can NEVER produce this marker

GAP

Missing critical information. Do not try to fill it — flag it for research.

  • Information needed but not available
  • Use sparingly; each gap forces action

INFERENCE

Assumption declared. User must accept or reject.

  • Logical leap from confirmed facts
  • Extrapolation from limited data
  • State “Risk if wrong: [impact]”

UNVERIFIED

Could not verify, or Tier 4 source only.

  • Self-reported data
  • Never present as fact

RISK

Reputational, legal, or credibility flag.

  • Outdated info as current
  • Sensitive claims without strong source
  • Triggers Devil’s Advocate review

◆ Source Tier Classification

Four-tier hierarchy. Higher tier always wins in conflicts. Recency breaks ties within same tier.

TIER 1 Primary / Authoritative

High confidence — use as primary evidence.

  • SEC filings, government databases, peer-reviewed journals
  • Court documents, audited financials
  • Official entity press releases

TIER 2 Reputable Secondary

Good confidence — verify if critical.

  • FT, WSJ, Reuters, BBC
  • Gartner, McKinsey, Forrester
  • Bloomberg, FactSet

TIER 3 General Secondary

Moderate — cross-reference recommended.

  • News outlets, expert blogs
  • Company marketing sites
  • Wikipedia (context only)

TIER 4 Unverified / Low Quality

Low — NEVER mark as CONFIRMED.

  • Forums, social media, anonymous blogs
  • Content farms, commercial bias
  • Outdated content (>2 years, fast sectors)

◆ Data & Analytics Rules

Anti-hallucination rules for metrics, KPIs, and analytical content.

Verification Standards

  • Statistical claims: disclose methodology
  • Percentages: specify base/denominator
  • YoY comparisons: consistent periods
  • Currency: specify + nominal/real
  • Growth: distinguish CAGR vs. point-to-point
  • Trends: minimum 3 data points

Hallucination Patterns

PatternRule
Invented precisionMatch source (“~24%” not “23.7%”)
Conflated metricsMAU ≠ DAU — define once
Cherry-picked timeShow full-year context
Survivorship bias“Among those that survived”
Correlation ≠ cause“Correlates with” never “caused”
Missing denominatorAlways include base population
Zombie statisticsFlag if >2 years old

◆ Consulting Etiquette — 7 Golden Rules

Your personal reputation IS your firm’s reputation.

1. Never Speak Ill

Criticism damages your credibility more than the target’s.

2. Never Complain

Reframe problems as opportunities. Clients hire solvers.

3. Never Compromise Clients

Confidentiality survives the end of the engagement.

4. Protect the Brand

You represent your firm 24/7.

5. Client Right (Publicly)

Keep disagreements private. United front.

6. Never Burn Bridges

Everyone today could be a client tomorrow.

7. Compete with Class

Win on merit, never undermine others.
